What is the Syrian diaspora?

Van Hear's (2009) concept of diaspora offers a useful lens through which to understand the Syrian refugee experience, revealing three key interconnected entities. First, (1) the homeland itself presents a tragic reality: while many Syrians sought refuge elsewhere, countless others remained within its borders, often in precarious situations. Refugee camps, concentrated in northwest Syria, became home for those unable to leave, who often chose the hardship of tent life over the dangers of the Al-Assad regime. Even after its fall, the devastation of their homes left many reliant on these temporary shelters. Second, (2) the neighboring countries—Turkey, Lebanon, Egypt, Jordan, and Iraq—became the initial destinations for many fleeing Syrians. Jordan's Za'atari camp stands as a stark reminder, being both the largest Syrian refugee camp and one of the world's most extensive. Third, (3) the broader diaspora encompasses the global dispersal of Syrians beyond these immediate neighbors. With significant populations in EU member states like Germany (around one million), this entity plays a crucial role in the overall diaspora dynamic. Crucially, the relationships between these three parts are vital. As Van Hear (2003) notes, these connections facilitate a flow of diverse support—emotional, financial, and informational. This interconnectedness is particularly significant for Syrians, who often rely on these links for emotional support, crucial information, and, for those within Syria, vital financial assistance from relatives and friends abroad. The dire economic situation within Syria, with UNHCR estimating 97 percent poverty by the end of 2021, underscores the critical role of these informal financial networks, which have accumulated due to the international sanctions imposed on Syria during Bashar Al-Assad's rule and the corruption of the latter. Community Building vs. Reach Strategy

Community building is a powerful tool for engaging with your followers, current audience, and targeted audience. It is crucial for expanding your network and enhancing the legitimacy of your efforts. Many advocacy groups and organizations focus solely on reaching policymakers, politicians, and people in power, often at the expense of a far more critical segment: the people they advocate for. They rely on a "one-way ticket" strategy, a traditional, top-down, and one-way communication approach, forgetting how essential the very people they represent are in giving them power and legitimacy. These groups fail to recognize that people are critical thinkers who hold real power. When people are excluded from the conversation, they withhold their support and remain unaware of the fantastic initiatives being done on their behalf. In turn, these advocacy groups' media channels attract zero interaction and become similar to identically designed megaphones, each broadcasting the same message: "We met, we discussed, we decided." When the lack of engagement is finally realized, a lot of effort and money is spent to fix the situation. However, these efforts often deepen the gap because they continue to follow the same fashion: they aim at reaching the same targeted audience of policymakers, politicians, and people in power. As a consequence, these organizations enter a loop of trying to increase interaction. This goal appears in their communication strategy every year, but with the same unrealized outcome. While they attempt to solve the issue, they often deploy a short-term strategy that overwhelms the public with paid ads focused on reach. As soon as the campaign is over, everything goes back to normal with no actual outcome beyond a small, temporary increase in numbers.

The five models of media structure

Understanding the five models of media structural organization is crucial for companies and organizations of all sizes. Whether a business is small with limited resources or a large corporation, rethinking communication strategies can lead to significant improvements. Choosing a specific communication model requires minimal effort yet can yield a huge impact. However, this decision must be made carefully, considering the team’s capacity, organizational needs, and strategic objectives, as each model comes with its own advantages and challenges. The key is not only to adopt a model but also to find ways to mitigate its weaknesses and turn them into strengths. Small organizations and associations often rely on one person to handle all communication tasks, including social media, content creation, and media outreach. To optimize resources, they also outsource tasks to media agencies for social media campaigns, creative design, and event coverage. This setup aligns closely with the centralized model, which provides greater control over messaging and brand consistency. However, this model has risks: Repetitive and unengaging content – Relying too much on automation or rigid brand guidelines can lead to generic, uninspiring posts. Lack of interaction – Content that does not encourage audience engagement can result in low impact and reach. Creativity limitations – Even attempts to introduce interactive content may not always succeed in fixing engagement issues if the strategy remains overly rigid. Selecting the right model ensures that communication efforts do not become chaotic or turn into repetitive routines with no added value. Unfortunately, many organizations treat communication activities—such as website updates and social media posts—as routine tasks, rather than strategic opportunities to connect with their audience. This approach fails to generate engagement and does not effectively target the intended audience. The Role of Hybrid Models & AI in Modern Communication Choosing a communication model not only structures internal efforts but also shapes relationships with the public and impacts organizational goals. Organizations do not need to limit themselves to one specific model—a hybrid approach can offer greater flexibility. Additionally, leveraging AI-supported tools can enhance communication strategies, improve audience insights, and optimize content for engagement. By combining structured models with innovative tools, organizations can ensure effective, engaging, and impactful communication efforts.
